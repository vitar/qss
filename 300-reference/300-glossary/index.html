    Glossary¶

    Short, practical definitions of terms used across the Question Space System (QSS).

    You can skim this once or jump back here when something in Theory or Practice feels fuzzy.

    Core System Terms¶

    Question Space¶

    A designed structure of inquiry for a specific situation.

    A question space is:

    • not just a list of questions,
    • but a combination of:
    • Orientation (what this is for, for whom, under which constraints),
    • Topology (which dimensions of inquiry are active),
    • Flow (order, depth, branching, entry/exit),
    • Recursion (how the space learns from its own use).

    It is always:

    • context-specific,
    • power-aware,
    • and bounded in scope (not therapy, not legal or medical work, not emergency response).

    Question Space System (QSS)¶

    The whole framework described in this repository:

    • Theory:
    • concepts, architecture, dimensions, modes, patterns.
    • Practice:
    • methods, quickstarts, domain playbooks.
    • Reference:
    • glossary, templates, examples.

    QSS is meant to help you:

    • design,
    • use,
    • and evolve

    your own question spaces for the domains you care about.

    Orientation¶

    The explicit statement of:

    • what this space is for,
    • who it is for and who is affected,
    • which mode you are in (Setup, Stabilization, Growth),
    • how power and safety look,
    • what is in scope and out of scope.

    Orientation is the starting point for every serious question space.
    If Orientation is wrong, everything built on top of it is distorted.

    Topology¶

    The choice of which dimensions of inquiry are active in a given space.

    Examples:

    • Clarification,
    • Assumptions,
    • Boundaries & Ownership,
    • Emotion & Needs,
    • Evidence & Uncertainty,
    • Meta, and others.

    Topology answers:

    • “Which lenses do we apply here, and which do we deliberately leave out?”

    Flow¶

    The ordered path through a question space:

    • Entry:
    • how you begin.
    • Sequence:
    • which dimensions you visit and in what order.
    • Depth control:
    • how far you go in each dimension.
    • Branching:
    • how you adapt to what emerges.
    • Exit:
    • how you close and integrate.

    Flow is where a topology becomes a usable conversation.

    Recursion¶

    The space reflecting on itself.

    In practice, recursion means:

    • In-session Meta:
    • checking how the conversation is landing.
    • Post-session review:
    • noting what worked, what didn’t.
    • Pattern-level iteration:
    • updating the design, versioning spaces over time.

    Recursion turns QSS into a learning system instead of a fixed set of scripts.

    Mode¶

    The overall stance of a question space relative to the situation:

    • Setup:
    • designing or initiating something before it breaks.
    • Stabilization:
    • repairing or stabilizing a situation that is slipping or tense.
    • Growth:
    • deepening, optimizing, or expanding something that basically works.

    Mode strongly influences which dimensions and flows are appropriate.

    Context¶

    The real-world situation the space is applied to:

    • domain:
    • work, consulting, personal, creative, etc.
    • stakes:
    • low, medium, high.
    • time constraints:
    • quick chat vs workshop vs self-reflection.
    • format:
    • 1:1, group, async, written, live.

    Context is captured during intake and summarized in Orientation.

    Participant¶

    Any person or role that takes part in or is directly affected by the question space.

    Examples:

    • client sponsor, vendor PM, tech lead, team members, band members, yourself.

    Participants can have different levels of power, risk, and ability to speak freely.

    Power and Safety¶

    Short-hand for:

    • who has more formal or informal authority,
    • who is more exposed to risk (job, contract, reputation, health),
    • how safe it is to speak honestly in this space.

    QSS treats power and safety as structural facts, not personal flaws.
    They affect:

    • which questions are safe to ask,
    • how Emotion & Needs is phrased,
    • and how Meta questions are used.

    Scope and Limits¶

    The boundaries of what a question space is meant to handle.

    QSS is designed for:

    • reflection,
    • alignment,
    • sense-making,
    • decision support in work and creative contexts.

    QSS is not designed for:

    • emergency situations,
    • diagnosis or treatment (medical or mental health),
    • legal advice or disputes,
    • deep therapeutic processing of trauma.

    When a space touches these areas, the responsible move is to:

    • stop or lighten the inquiry,
    • and consider redirecting to appropriate support.

    Dimensions of Inquiry¶

    These are the main “lenses” you can activate in a topology.

    Clarification¶

    Goal:

    • align on meaning and basic facts.

    Used for:

    • “What are we even talking about?”
    • “What problem are we trying to solve?”

    Typical questions:

    • “In your own words, what is this about?”
    • “What does this term mean to you?”

    Assumptions¶

    Goal:

    • surface what is taken for granted but not stated.

    Used for:

    • uncovering hidden expectations,
    • explaining repeated surprises or disappointments.

    Typical questions:

    • “What were you expecting that we never said out loud?”
    • “What are we treating as fixed that might not be?”

    Boundaries and Ownership¶

    Goal:

    • define scope, responsibilities, and decision rights.

    Used for:

    • clarifying who decides what,
    • reducing “work that falls between chairs”,
    • limiting over-responsibility.

    Typical questions:

    • “Who owns this decision?”
    • “Where do handoffs feel risky or unclear?”

    Value and Impact¶

    Goal:

    • understand what matters and for whom.

    Used for:

    • aligning priorities,
    • making trade-offs visible.

    Typical questions:

    • “Who is most affected if this goes well or badly?”
    • “What does success look like, and for whom?”

    Emotional and Relational Field / Needs¶

    Goal:

    • make the felt experience and basic needs visible enough to design responsibly.

    Used for:

    • situations with tension, fatigue, enthusiasm, or fear,
    • aligning collaboration with human needs.

    Constraints:

    • stays in the context of this work/relationship/creative project and near future,
    • avoids deep clinical or therapeutic work.

    Typical questions:

    • “How does this collaboration feel right now?”
    • “Which of your needs are clearly met or not met here?”

    Contrast and Alternatives¶

    Goal:

    • compare options and highlight trade-offs.

    Used for:

    • avoiding false binaries,
    • mapping different plausible paths.

    Typical questions:

    • “What are the main paths we see?”
    • “What does each path give and cost us?”

    Causality and Consequence¶

    Goal:

    • connect causes and effects, especially in recurring patterns.

    Used for:

    • understanding why certain dynamics repeat,
    • anticipating consequences of choices.

    Typical questions:

    • “What usually happens next when this pattern starts?”
    • “If we choose this option, what likely chain of events follows?”

    Layering / Abstraction¶

    Goal:

    • move between levels of detail and system views.

    Used for:

    • keeping conversations from getting stuck in tiny details or vague generalities.

    Typical questions:

    • “At the high level, what are we trying to do?”
    • “Can you give one concrete example of that?”

    Temporal¶

    Goal:

    • use time as a lens (past, present, future).

    Used for:

    • spotting trends,
    • imagining consequences,
    • extracting lessons.

    Typical questions:

    • “When did this start?”
    • “If nothing changes in six or twelve months, how will that feel?”

    Evidence and Uncertainty¶

    Goal:

    • distinguish what is known from what is guessed,
    • gauge confidence and risk.

    Used for:

    • any decision with stakes,
    • correcting overconfident narratives.

    Typical questions:

    • “What do we actually know from data or experience?”
    • “What are we assuming, and how could we be wrong?”
    • “What experiment would reduce our uncertainty?”

    Meta / Reflexive¶

    Goal:

    • reflect on the space itself and how it is working.

    Used for:

    • recursion,
    • co-steering the conversation.

    Typical questions:

    • “What feels useful or not useful about this conversation?”
    • “Have we named the real issue, or are we circling?”

    Patterns and Flows¶

    Shallow Pass¶

    A first light pass over multiple dimensions:

    • one or two questions per dimension,
    • to see where the energy or problem really is.

    Often followed by a deep dive into one or two dimensions.

    Deep Dive¶

    Focused exploration of a specific dimension:

    • used after a shallow pass reveals where the leverage is,
    • or when entering a space with a very clear problem area.

    Branching Flow¶

    A flow with conditional paths:

    • Core trunk:
    • a few questions you always ask.
    • Branches:
    • additional questions you use only if certain signals appear:
      • strong disagreement,
      • visible frustration,
      • conflicting facts.

    Ladder Flow¶

    A deliberate alternation between:

    • abstract questions (principles, patterns, big picture),
    • concrete questions (examples, specific incidents).

    Used to avoid getting stuck at only one level.

    Funnel Flow¶

    A pattern that moves from:

    • wide exploration of values and options,
    • through analysis of consequences and evidence,
    • toward narrower commitment and next steps.

    Modes in Practice¶

    Setup Mode¶

    You are:

    • designing or initiating something new,
    • trying to prevent obvious misalignment,
    • setting expectations and boundaries.

    Key dimensions often include:

    • Clarification,
    • Assumptions,
    • Boundaries & Ownership,
    • Value & Impact,
    • Evidence & Uncertainty.

    Stabilization Mode¶

    You are:

    • dealing with drift, conflict, or tension,
    • trying to stop things from getting worse,
    • aiming for small stabilizing moves.

    Key dimensions often include:

    • Clarification,
    • Temporal,
    • Assumptions,
    • Boundaries & Ownership,
    • Emotion & Needs,
    • Value & Impact,
    • Evidence & Uncertainty.

    Growth Mode¶

    You are:

    • improving something that already basically works,
    • exploring options and trade-offs,
    • designing experiments or long-term direction.

    Key dimensions often include:

    • Value & Impact,
    • Contrast & Alternatives,
    • Temporal,
    • Causality & Consequence,
    • Layering,
    • Evidence & Uncertainty,
    • Meta.

    Domain-Specific Terms (as used in Playbooks)¶

    These are not universal definitions; they are how QSS uses them in the domain playbooks.

    Client–Vendor Engagement¶

    Any relationship where:

    • one party buys or commissions work,
    • the other delivers services or products.

    In QSS:

    • often a focus area for Setup and Stabilization spaces,
    • with explicit attention to power, contracts, and expectations.

    DevEx (Developer Experience)¶

    The lived experience of engineers in an organization:

    • tools,
    • processes,
    • culture,
    • cognitive load,
    • ability to do good work without excessive friction.

    In QSS:

    • often a Growth-mode topic in delivery/consulting playbooks.

    Band Identity¶

    The shared sense of:

    • what a band or musical project sounds like,
    • what it stands for,
    • what listeners can expect.

    In QSS:

    • a focus area for creative Growth-mode spaces (music playbook).

    Mixing Direction¶

    The deliberate choice of:

    • sonic aesthetic,
    • balance between raw/live vs polished/studio,
    • intended listening context.

    In QSS:

    • often explored via Contrast & Alternatives, Value & Impact, Emotion & Needs.

    Safety and Ethics¶

    Emotional Scope¶

    The intended depth of emotional exploration:

    • present-focused,
    • context-bound (work, collaboration, creative project),
    • near-term future.

    QSS does not aim to process:

    • deep childhood history,
    • trauma,
    • or clinical conditions.

    Red Flags¶

    Signals that QSS should step back and other support should step in:

    • risk of harm or self-harm,
    • serious health symptoms,
    • legal threats or disputes,
    • abuse or harassment.

    When these appear, the appropriate move is to:

    • pause or limit the question space,
    • acknowledge the weight,
    • suggest or seek more suitable help.

    You do not need to memorize any of this.

    The glossary is here so that when a term appears in Theory, Practice, or examples, you can quickly:

    • check what it means in this system,
    • and decide how you want to adapt it in your own Question Space System.
